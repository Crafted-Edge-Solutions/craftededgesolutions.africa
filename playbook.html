Design Playbook.
How we make
websites feel premium.

A transferable set of principles — not templates — that we apply across every brand we work on. The same ideology that shapes craftededgesolutions.africa can produce a completely different result for a wellness brand, a fintech, or a law firm. The DNA travels; the surface adapts.

— 01

Lead with capability, not geography.

Where you're based goes near the bottom — not the headline. Strong positioning anchors the reader in what you do before locating where you are.

This isn't about hiding origin. It's about ordering perception correctly. Reader thinks "what can they do for me?" before "where are they?" — every time.

— Defensive
"Nairobi-based technology consultancy offering end-to-end solutions for businesses across Africa."
— Confident
"A product engineering studio building software, AI systems, and infrastructure. Operating globally. Built in East Africa."
— 02

One brand. One accent.

Every premium brand commits to one accent colour and uses it sparingly — for emphasis, status, and the occasional italic. Everything else lives in a monochrome family: one neutral, three text tones, two surface tones, two line tones.

The accent is a highlighter, not a paint roller. Used 4–6 times per page, max. The eye is drawn exactly where you want it.

Wellness brands choose sage or terracotta. Fintech picks one electric blue. We picked the orange in our logo. Same principle — different colour.

— 03

Editorial scale.

Hero text breaks past 6rem. Section heads land at 3–4rem. Body sits at 1rem. The leap between display and body is intentional — it's what makes the page feel like a magazine, not a brochure.

Small type is fine in service of large type. Mono labels at 0.7rem read as captions when the heading above them is at 4rem. The ratio is the rhythm.

Rule: if your h1 looks like your h2, both are wrong.

— 04

Three type families.
Three roles.

The system is always three voices:

— Display
Serif. Editorial.
Heads, hero, italic emphasis. Instrument Serif here. Fraunces / Cormorant elsewhere.
— Body
Sans. Neutral. Inter.
Paragraphs, descriptions. Should disappear. Inter, DM Sans, Geist all work.
— Label
— Mono. Caps.
Eyebrows, stat labels, nav, status. Adds technical texture.

Each one has a job. Don't make body do display work, don't make mono do body work.

— 05

Numbers. Not icons.

Numbered sections (— 01, — 02) signal order, hierarchy, and seriousness. Icons signal mood. Most B2B work needs the former and gets sabotaged by the latter.

Emoji feel cheerful. Coloured icons feel templated. Numbers feel like a document — something considered. Whoever is reading came for substance; give them structure.

Exception: when the brand IS playful (a kids' product, a creative agency, a music app), icons earn their place. For anything that wants to feel like infrastructure, premium service, or professional craft — use numbers.

— 06

Borders over cards.

Most websites use raised cards (drop shadow, rounded corner, background colour) to group content. That instinct is correct but the execution is heavy. Lines do the same job with one-tenth the visual weight.

A 1px line between cells reads as a grid. A floating card with shadow reads as a Bootstrap site. Same hierarchy, completely different feel.

Use cards sparingly — only when content needs to be lifted (a CTA, a form, a quote). Everything else: lines.

— 07

Frame positively.

Telling readers what you're not still anchors them on the thing you don't want them thinking about. The brain registers "we don't do generic agency work" as "agency work" — then "not". You've made them think about agency work.

Lead with the affirmative. Let the reader infer the contrast.

— Negative anchor
"We don't sell you one product. We map out what your business needs and build the right combination of tools that actually solve your problem."
— Positive frame
"Every engagement starts in the same place — your business — and ends with a complete operating system built around it."
— 08

Motion with restraint.

Smooth scroll, fade-ups, hero reveals, magnetic buttons, soft cursor — all of these add a sense of being made by people. But they only work when used once per page section, no more. Motion fatigues fast.

The rules:

  • — Animations should feel like a fade, not a performance.
  • — Easing matters more than duration. Use cubic-bezier(0.22, 1, 0.36, 1) for almost everything.
  • — Honor prefers-reduced-motion. Always.
  • — If you can't tell whether motion is needed, it isn't.
— 09

Hierarchy through spacing.

Generous space tells the reader "this matters, slow down." Compressed space says "form filling, get through it fast." Most websites are too dense because designers fear empty space.

Our defaults: sections breathe at 100–160px vertical padding. Between heading and body: 24px. Between sub-points: 18px. Inside a card: 24–32px. Outside on mobile: 22–32px. Outside on desktop: 32–48px.

White (or dark) space is content. It's saying something. Use it deliberately.

— 10

Earn every element.

The hardest principle to follow. Most websites are bloated because every team member added "one more thing" — an FAQ section, a testimonials row, a coming-soon banner, a newsletter strip. Each one diluted the page.

Before any element ships, ask: "What does this earn?" If the answer is "it fills space," cut it. A page with five things that matter is more valuable than ten things that almost matter.

This is why this playbook has ten principles and not fifty. Most rules are nuance under these ten.

— Adaptation

Same DNA.
Different brand.

How the same ten principles produce two completely different sites — one for a software studio, one for a wellness brand. The execution shifts. The discipline doesn't.

— Photography

Crafted EdgeNone — pure typography, no imagery. The work is the demo.

— Pace

Crafted EdgeQuick, sharp transitions. Smooth scroll but tight reveals.
Wellness SolutionsSlower easing. Longer holds. The site should feel like breath.

— Copy voice

Crafted EdgeDirect, opinionated, technical. "Engineering that ships."
Wellness SolutionsWarm, considered, sensory. "Care that's actually felt."

— What stays the same

Both sitesNumbered sections. Em-dash eyebrows. Three type families. One accent. Editorial scale. Borders over cards. Positive framing. Generous space.

— Implementation

Wagtail
+ Django
specifics.

  • — 01 · Lock the palette in base.html

    Define CSS custom properties at :root in your base template. Every page extends from this. The CMS shouldn't be able to override colours — that's how brand consistency dies.

  • — 02 · Build StreamField blocks that enforce the system

    Don't give editors a RichTextBlock with full HTML access. Build typed blocks: HeroBlock, NumberedListBlock, StatGridBlock, PullQuoteBlock. Each one renders to the design system. Editors can't break the design because they can't access it.

  • — 03 · One template per block — and that's the law

    templates/blocks/hero.html, templates/blocks/numbered_list.html, etc. Each template is the design — it can't be overridden by content. The CMS controls what appears, not how.

  • — 04 · Restrict image uploads with editorial guidance

    Add help_text to image fields telling editors what kind of photo to use ("Editorial portraits, soft natural light, single subject"). Crop them with Wagtail's focal point. Keep aspect ratios consistent. A bad photo is worse than no photo.

  • — 05 · Static motion layer, CMS-independent

    Smooth scroll, reveals, magnetic buttons, custom cursor — these live in static/js/motion.js and apply globally. The CMS doesn't touch them. Editors get the polish for free.

  • — 06 · Performance is part of the design

    Use {% image %} for responsive images with format-webp. Lazy-load below the fold. Inline critical CSS. A slow site doesn't feel premium no matter how it's designed.
